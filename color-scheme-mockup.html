Overview

What the research tells us

SETD5 Syndrome is caused by a change in the SETD5 gene, which plays a key role in how genes are turned on and off during brain development. Most cases arise from a new (de novo) variant, not inherited from either parent.

Consistent findings

  • Intellectual disability (mild–moderate)
  • Speech and language delays
  • Autism spectrum features
  • Behavioral challenges

Variable features

  • Seizures (~30% of cases)
  • Feeding difficulties in infancy
  • Subtle facial features
  • Low muscle tone

2024
Peer-reviewed
Longitudinal outcomes in SETD5 haploinsufficiency: a cohort of 84 individuals
Yamamoto et al. · Genetics in Medicine · 2024
The largest cohort study to date, following 84 individuals with confirmed SETD5 variants over 5 years. Researchers tracked developmental trajectories, medical complications, and response to early interventions.
What this means for families Children who received speech therapy before age 3 showed significantly better communication outcomes at age 8, reinforcing the importance of early intervention.
Open access · DOI: 10.1038/gim.2024.xxx
Review
Chromatin regulation in neurodevelopmental disorders: lessons from SETD5
Chen & Park · Nature Reviews Neuroscience · 2024
A comprehensive review placing SETD5 in the broader landscape of chromatin-regulating genes implicated in intellectual disability, comparing mechanisms with KDM5C, KMT2A, and related genes.
Subscription required · DOI: 10.1038/nrn.2024.xxx
Emerging research

Clinical trials on the horizon

Two preclinical studies published in 2023 identified potential therapeutic targets in mouse models. A Phase 1 trial is expected to open enrollment in late 2026. Read more →

Knowledge Gaps

What we still don't know

Long-term adult outcomes — The oldest described cohorts are now in their late 20s. Systematic adult outcome data doesn't yet exist.
Genotype-phenotype correlation — It remains unclear why individuals with similar variants show such wide variation in severity.
Seizure management — No controlled studies have compared antiepileptic approaches in SETD5-specific seizure profiles.

How we arrived at the diagnosis

Our journey to diagnosis took 11 years. Along the way, there were countless appointments, unanswered questions, medical mysteries, and a lot of watching and waiting. Friends and family often reassured us that he'd probably outgrow it, but we always knew deep down that something more was going on. We had microarray genetic testing done when my son was eight, but a microarray gene test wouldn't have been able to detect that small single-gene change, so it showed no issues. Fast forward three years, and the turning point came when a kind doctor finally took our repeated concerns seriously. He really listened and sent referrals that led to EEGs, bone scans, and ultimately more in-depth genetic testing. That testing gave us the answer we had always known was there: SETD5 Syndrome, a rare neurodevelopmental disorder caused by a mutation in the SETD5 gene.

Advice right after diagnosis

Trust yourself. The self-doubt you felt along the way was completely normal, but your instincts were right, and pushing for that testing was absolutely the right call. Keep listening to that inner voice, because no one knows your child better than you do. And give yourself grace for the hard days, because there will be some. But I promise you, there will be many, many good ones too.

What this life has taught us

SETD5 Syndrome has taught me how important advocacy is. Because the condition is so rare, there isn't much information out there yet, and that means it falls to families like ours to push for answers, seek out guidance, and share what we learn along the way. We are all building this road together. It has also taught me so much about my son. He has real challenges as a result of this rare condition, but he is also strong, kind, creative, and funny, and his way of thinking outside the box never ceases to amaze me. His resilience is truly inspiring, and he surprises me in the best possible ways, more times than I can count. It's amazing to read the research and see them actually highlight the 'happy disposition' that many people with SETD5 Syndrome share.

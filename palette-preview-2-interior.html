Understanding Your Genetic Report

Family Toolkit · Updated 2025
For parents and caregivers

Your child's genetic test report is full of specialized language. This page breaks down each section of a typical SETD5 report so you know exactly what you're looking at — no science background needed.

Gene
SETD5
+

This is the gene where the lab found a change. SETD5 provides instructions for making a protein involved in brain development and gene regulation. It sits on chromosome 3 at position 3p25.3. Changes in this gene are associated with intellectual disability and developmental delays - a condition sometimes called SETD5 Syndrome or MRD23.

Variant
c.2025_2026delAG (p.Gly676Valfs*2)
+

The exact "address" of the change in the DNA. The c. notation tells the lab where in the gene the change occurred. The p. notation describes how the protein is affected. This is the key identifier when talking to a genetic counselor or searching databases like ClinVar.

Variant Type
Frameshift (deletion)
+

What kind of change happened to the DNA. Different types affect the gene in different ways. Your report will list one of the following:

Types of genetic changes seen in SETD5

Frameshift

A letter added or deleted, scrambling all instructions that follow. Like removing one letter from a sentence so every word after becomes garbled.

Nonsense

One letter changed to create a premature stop signal. The protein gets cut short and usually cannot function.

Missense

One letter changed, swapping one protein building block for another. The protein is still made but may not work correctly.

Splice-site

A change at the boundary where the gene's instructions are edited together. Can disrupt how the protein is assembled.

Deletion

A piece of the genetic code is missing - small (a few letters) or large (a section of chromosome).

Duplication

A section of genetic code is copied, appearing twice instead of once.

Classification
Likely Pathogenic
+

The lab's assessment of whether this change is responsible for your child's condition. Labs use a five-tier system defined by the ACMG (American College of Medical Genetics).

Inheritance
De novo (autosomal dominant)
+

Where the change came from and how it's passed through families. Most SETD5 cases are de novo - the change happened new in your child and was not inherited from either parent.

Zygosity
Heterozygous
+

How many copies of the gene are affected. You have two copies of most genes. Heterozygous means one is changed, one is working. This is expected for SETD5 Syndrome.

ACMG Criteria
PVS1, PM2, PP3
+

The specific evidence codes the lab used to arrive at the classification above. Each code represents one piece of evidence. These are primarily for clinicians and genetic counselors.

Interpreting your results

What the Classification Means

Pathogenic

This change is known to cause disease. Strong evidence from multiple sources. The clearest result a genetic test can give.

Likely Pathogenic

Strong evidence this change causes disease, but seen in fewer people. Most clinicians treat this the same as Pathogenic.

Variant of Uncertain Significance

Not enough evidence yet. Doesn't confirm or rule out a diagnosis. Can be reclassified as more data becomes available.

Likely Benign / Benign

Evidence suggests this change does not cause disease. Common in the general population and not associated with your child's condition.

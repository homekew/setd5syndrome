What is SETD5 Syndrome?

SETD5 Syndrome is a rare genetic neurodevelopmental condition caused by a change (variant) in the SETD5 gene on chromosome 3. This gene helps regulate how other genes are switched on and off during development. When one copy does not function correctly, it can lead to a range of developmental, behavioral, medical, and physical features. No two individuals are exactly alike. The condition affects each person differently, and severity varies widely.

Commonly Reported Features (not every individual will have all of these)
Learning & Cognition
  • Intellectual disability (mild to moderate)
  • Global developmental delay
  • Working memory difficulties
  • Uneven cognitive profile
  • Challenges with reading, writing, numeracy
Speech & Language
  • Speech and language delay (commonly reported)
  • Expressive language more affected than receptive
  • Some minimally verbal or nonverbal
  • Speech dysfluency, articulation difficulties
  • May benefit from AAC
Behavior & Emotional Regulation
  • ADHD features (inattention, hyperactivity)
  • Anxiety; difficulty with transitions
  • Behavioral rigidity; preference for routine
  • Autism spectrum features
  • Repetitive movements or behaviors
  • Sensory sensitivities
Social & Adaptive
  • Social communication challenges
  • Difficulty with awareness of danger
  • Often described as happy and sociable
Neurological & Motor
  • Low muscle tone (hypotonia)
  • Gross and fine motor delays
  • Coordination difficulties
  • Epilepsy or seizures
  • Sleep disturbances
Medical & Physical
  • Feeding difficulties in infancy
  • Growth concerns; short stature
  • Eye or vision concerns
  • Hearing impairment
  • Congenital heart defect
  • Musculoskeletal concerns
Inheritance & Recurrence

Most cases are de novo (a new mutation, not inherited from either parent). In some families, a parent carries the same variant and may pass it on. Parental genetic testing is often offered after a SETD5 diagnosis. If neither parent carries the variant, recurrence risk is very low. If a parent carries it, risk may be up to 50%. Genetic counseling is available through medical genetics programs and can help clarify recurrence risk and testing options for your family.

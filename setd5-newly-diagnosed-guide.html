The basics

What Is SETD5 Syndrome?

SETD5 Syndrome is a rare genetic neurodevelopmental disorder caused by a change (variant) in the SETD5 gene on chromosome 3. This gene regulates how other genes are switched on and off during development. When one copy doesn't function correctly, it can lead to a range of developmental, behavioral, medical, and physical features ‚Äî no two individuals are exactly alike. See the Symptoms & Features tab for details. The condition was first described in medical literature in 2014.

You may see it listed under several names in medical records or research papers: SETD5 Syndrome (most common among families), SETD5 haploinsufficiency (the clinical term), MRD23 (an older classification code), and Intellectual Disability-Facial Dysmorphism Syndrome due to SETD5 Haploinsufficiency (the formal diagnostic name). All refer to the same condition.

Disorder, syndrome, or disease?

You may hear SETD5 described as all three ‚Äî and technically, each is correct. A syndrome describes a recognizable pattern of features that tend to appear together, which is why "SETD5 Syndrome" is the most widely used name. A disorder refers to how the condition disrupts typical development and functioning ‚Äî "neurodevelopmental disorder" is the clinical category it falls under, alongside conditions like autism and ADHD. A disease is a broader medical term for any condition with a specific, identifiable cause ‚Äî SETD5 qualifies because it has a known genetic origin. Most families and clinicians use "syndrome" or "disorder" in everyday conversation, but you may encounter "disease" in research papers or insurance contexts.

How it happens

What Caused This?

In the vast majority of cases, SETD5 mutations occur de novo ‚Äî meaning they arise spontaneously and are not passed down from either parent. It is a random change in a single gene that occurred very early in development.

In a minority of families, however, one parent may carry the same genetic change and pass it on to their child. This is why parental genetic testing is recommended after a diagnosis ‚Äî to determine whether the variant is de novo or inherited, which affects the recurrence risk for future pregnancies.

Recurrence risk

If neither parent carries the variant, the chance of having another affected child is very low ‚Äî though not zero, since a parent can carry the change in only some of their egg or sperm cells (known as germline mosaicism). If a parent is found to carry the same variant, the chance of recurrence can be as high as 50%. A genetic counselor can give you specific guidance for your family's situation.

SETD5 works by adding chemical "tags" to histones ‚Äî the proteins that DNA wraps around. These tags help regulate which genes are active. When SETD5 doesn't function properly in one copy, the balance of gene expression during brain development is disrupted. This is called haploinsufficiency ‚Äî one working copy of the gene isn't enough to carry out its full function.

What does that mean in plain language?

Think of SETD5 as a volume knob for development ‚Äî it helps turn genes up or down at the right moments. Everyone has two copies of this knob. In SETD5 Syndrome, one knob is broken. The single working copy can't keep everything properly tuned, so some genes get switched on or off at the wrong time during development. That's what haploinsufficiency means: one copy isn't enough.

Diagnosis

How Is It Diagnosed?

SETD5 Syndrome is diagnosed through genetic testing. Many families receive the diagnosis after years of searching for answers, a process sometimes called a "diagnostic odyssey." If you are newly diagnosed, know that the testing has now given you a specific answer, which opens the door to targeted support and appropriate specialists.

Common testing methods

  • Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)
  • Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)
  • Chromosomal Microarray (CMA)
  • Neurodevelopmental or Intellectual Disability Gene Panel
  • Trio Testing (child + both parents)

What the result means

  • A pathogenic or likely pathogenic variant in SETD5 ‚Äî this confirms a diagnosis
  • Some individuals receive a variant of uncertain significance (VUS) in SETD5 ‚Äî this means the variant has been identified but there isn't yet enough evidence to confirm whether it causes the condition; this classification can change over time as more data is collected
  • May be a point mutation or a larger deletion of the region
  • De novo in most cases (new, not inherited)
  • Can be confirmed with parental testing

What to know

Symptoms & Features

SETD5 Syndrome affects each person differently. Some individuals have many of the features listed below; others have only a few. Severity varies widely ‚Äî even among people with the same type of SETD5 variant. No single feature is present in 100% of individuals.

Important note

This list describes features that have been reported across individuals with SETD5 Syndrome. It is not a prediction of what your child or you will experience. Bring any specific questions to your medical team.

Core features

Neurodevelopmental & Behavioral

Learning & Cognition

  • Intellectual disability (mild to moderate in most; some borderline or near-typical)
  • Global developmental delay
  • Difficulties with reading, writing, and numeracy
  • "Spiky" cognitive profile ‚Äî strengths in some areas alongside challenges in others
  • Working memory and short-term memory difficulties
  • Long-term memory may be relatively stronger

Speech & Language

  • Speech and language delay (nearly universal)
  • Expressive language typically more affected than receptive
  • Some individuals are minimally verbal or nonverbal
  • Speech dysfluency (not a true stammer, but may sound similar)
  • Articulation and pronunciation difficulties
  • Benefits from AAC (augmentative and alternative communication)

Behavior & Emotional Regulation

  • ADHD features ‚Äî inattention, hyperactivity, impulsivity
  • Anxiety and difficulty with transitions or change
  • Behavioral rigidity; preference for routine
  • Aggression or behavioral outbursts in some
  • Autism spectrum disorder features (diagnosed in ~24% of reported cases)
  • Repetitive movements or behaviors
  • Obsessive compulsive behaviors in some
  • Sensory sensitivities are common in those with ASD features

Social & Adaptive

  • Social understanding and communication challenges
  • Difficulty with awareness of danger or personal safety
  • Limited independence with daily living skills in some
  • Emotional intelligence may be a relative strength
  • Many individuals are described as affectionate and sociable

Physical features

Neurological, Physical & Growth Features

Neurological & Motor

  • Low muscle tone (hypotonia) ‚Äî in ~39% of reported cases
  • Gait abnormalities, including tip-toe walking (~36%)
  • Coordination difficulties and clumsiness
  • Epilepsy or seizures (in approximately 14% of cases)
  • EEG abnormalities in some without clinical seizures
  • Motor development delays (gross and fine motor)
  • Sleep disturbances common

Craniofacial & Physical Features

  • Triangular-shaped face
  • Head size varies ‚Äî some children have a small head (microcephaly); head circumference should be monitored
  • Prominent eyebrows that meet or nearly meet (synophrys)
  • Widely spaced eyes (hypertelorism)
  • Strabismus (crossed or misaligned eyes)
  • Low-set or unusual ears
  • Nose with a wide base and tip
  • Long and smooth philtrum (groove between nose and upper lip)
  • Thin upper lip
  • High-arched palate
  • Teeth anomalies in some
  • Extra fingers or toes (polydactyly) ‚Äî reported in approximately 1 in 4 children
  • Features are typically mild and may be subtle

Feeding & Growth

  • Feeding difficulties in infancy (sucking, swallowing weakly)
  • Failure to thrive in some infants
  • Gastroesophageal reflux in some; surgery occasionally needed
  • Chewing difficulties common in older children
  • Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) in some ‚Äî should be monitored
  • Short stature; many remain small through childhood
  • Reduced bone mineral density (first described 2021)

Other Medical Features

  • Congenital heart defects in approximately 1 in 3 ‚Äî often an atrioventricular septal defect (AVSD); many do well after surgery
  • Eye and vision anomalies common ‚Äî including strabismus, nystagmus (involuntary eye movements), long- or short-sightedness, and ptosis (drooping eyelid)
  • Hearing impairment (temporary or permanent) in some ‚Äî can affect speech and language development
  • Cryptorchidism (undescended testes) and/or hypospadias in some males
  • Inguinal hernia in some
  • Musculoskeletal issues including scoliosis and joint laxity
  • Fragile bones reported in a small number
Variability

Not every person with SETD5 Syndrome will have all ‚Äî or even most ‚Äî of these features. Some individuals have relatively mild presentations; others face more significant challenges. The research literature is still growing, and our understanding of the full range of the condition continues to expand.

Getting started

What to Do After a Diagnosis

Receiving a diagnosis can feel overwhelming. It can also feel like a relief ‚Äî finally having a name for what you or your child has been experiencing. There is no single "right" path forward, but there are some clear first steps that can help you access the right support.

1

Request a genetics consultation

If you haven't already met with a medical geneticist or genetic counselor, this should be a priority. They can explain exactly what the variant means for your specific situation, clarify inheritance and recurrence risk, and help coordinate specialist referrals.

2

Connect with a developmental pediatrician

A developmental pediatrician can oversee your child's medical care, coordinate between specialists, and help identify what therapies and early interventions are most appropriate. For adults, a neurologist or adult geneticist may serve a similar coordinating role.

3

Access early intervention or school-based services

Children under age 3 may qualify for early intervention services through their state. School-age children may qualify for an IEP (Individualized Education Program) or 504 Plan. These services ‚Äî including speech, occupational, and physical therapy ‚Äî can make a significant difference and are provided at no cost through the public school system.

4

Ask about specialist referrals

At your first appointments, ask specifically which specialists your child should be referred to. Given the range of systems SETD5 Syndrome can affect, referrals to a neurologist, cardiologist, ophthalmologist, and audiologist are commonly recommended ‚Äî even when no obvious symptoms are present in those areas. See the Specialists to Consider section below for a full list of specialists and what each one addresses.

5

Enroll in a patient registry

Joining a research registry connects you with scientists studying SETD5 and may give you access to research studies. It also helps build the evidence base that will benefit future families. Simons Searchlight is the primary registry for SETD5.

6

Connect with the community

Other families living with SETD5 Syndrome are one of your most valuable resources. Online communities can offer practical advice, emotional support, and the perspective of people who understand what you're going through firsthand.

Your care team

Specialists to Consider

Medical Geneticist / Genetic Counselor

Explains the variant, inheritance, and recurrence risk. Often the first specialist after diagnosis.

Developmental Pediatrician

Oversees overall developmental care and coordinates specialists for children.

Neurologist

Evaluates and manages seizures, EEG findings, and neurological features. Recommended for all individuals. Ask your neurologist about screening for Moyamoya disease ‚Äî a rare condition affecting blood flow to the brain that has been reported in association with SETD5 Syndrome, and warrants discussion even if no symptoms are present.

Speech-Language Pathologist

Addresses speech, language, communication, and swallowing/feeding difficulties.

Occupational Therapist

Supports fine motor skills, daily living activities, and sensory processing.

Physical Therapist

Addresses gross motor delays, low muscle tone, gait, and musculoskeletal issues.

Psychologist / Behavioral Therapist

Supports behavior, anxiety, ASD features, and adaptive functioning.

Ophthalmologist

Evaluates strabismus and vision. Recommended early, especially if eye misalignment is present.

Audiologist

Tests hearing ‚Äî important given that hearing difficulties have been reported in some individuals.

Cardiologist

Evaluates for congenital heart defects if any cardiac concerns are present.

Orthopedics

Manages scoliosis, joint laxity, and other musculoskeletal issues if present.

Endocrinologist / Bone Specialist

Relevant if short stature or reduced bone density is a concern ‚Äî first documented in SETD5 in 2021.

Appointments

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What type of SETD5 variant does my child/I have ‚Äî a point mutation or a deletion? Does the type affect prognosis?
  • Is this de novo, or is there any chance it was inherited from one of the parents?
  • What is the recurrence risk for future pregnancies?
  • What specialists should we see first, given the features we're observing?
  • Should we do an EEG or cardiac evaluation, even if no seizures or heart symptoms are present?
  • Are there any clinical trials or research studies our family could participate in?
  • What should we be monitoring as our child grows ‚Äî milestones, bone density, vision, hearing?
  • What does this diagnosis mean for school services and IEP eligibility?
  • Is there a patient registry we should join?
